  • 06/2020
    DIY Monitor
    Monitor with speakers made from recycled parts
    As classes transitioned online in mid-March 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic, I suddenly needed to move back home and leave the monitor I used for school and work at Duke. At home, my parents had an old monitor that I could use as I completed the semester. However, I later found this monitor had a faulty power supply, sometimes working and sometimes immediately shutting down.

    Rather than purchasing a new monitor or new power supply to repair the monitor, I decided to salvage a screen from an old laptop lying around the house and build one myself. Additionally, I added speakers from an old broken TV also lying around the house. With a new LCD screen controller, a cheap amplifier, and some MDF board, I was able to build a fully functioning HDMI monitor with speakers. The entire build saved me money (totaling $30!) and gave me a fun mini project to do during a period of quarantine.

  • 02/2020 - 03/2020
    Responsive Heart
    Hardware designed for ECE 364: Performance and Technology
    One of the goals in Duke's ECE 364: Performance and Technology is to integrate hardware and software into performances. For our final project, groups were tasked with constructing a piece of technology and integrate into a performance that was heart-themed. Because of the COVID-19 pandemic, my group was unable to complete this project due to Duke's transition to remote learning. We later transitioned this project into a software version, which can be seen in the Software Projects page.

    Nevertheless, with the heart theme, my group wanted to evoke senses of wandering, anxiety, frustration, and end with a sense of relief. To do this, we created a story in which one wandering performer would try to find her "compatible match." She did this by scanning other performers who are have a small LED circuit that was in the shape of a heart. The wandering performer used a small wireless RGB color sensor to scan the other performers, and this sensor communicated to the central wooden heart via an Xbee serial communication protocol. The central wooden heart, which is a laser cut plywood that rotated around its axis via motors, would light up its LED strips based on the "compatibility" that was scanned. It would respond with different LED patterns and colors.

  • 01/2020 - 04/2020
    Mitt Mates
    Smart utility glove for DIY projects, home renovations, and more
    For my senior design project in Duke's ECE 496: Wearable & Ubiquitous Computing, my design group and I designed and constructed a smart utility glove that is useful for DIY projects, home renovations, and more.

    In the DIY and construciton industries, users need various tools that take up space, weigh a lot, and can hinder a user's overall workflow. Mitt Mates simplifies this workflow by integrating tools into the user's own work gloves. Each of the tools that we designed is its own stand-along module, giving the user freedom to put any combination of tools they need for the job onto their personal glove.

    The detachable glove modules that we designed include: distance measurement, flashlights, water flow/pipe clog detector, and a live wire sensor. We intended to include a stud finder, but failed to overcome some problems with its circuit over the course of the time constraint of the semester.   • 09/2018 - 03/2020
    EWH Patient Monitor
    Low-cost patient monitor with ECG and pulse oximeter modules
    In developing countries, one of the main problems in hospitals is constant brownouts, resulting in a disruption in power supply to hospitals. This can turn off key medical equipment such as patient monitors. Additionally, hospitals in less developed countries are not as well-funded as in developed countries, making it tough to get repairs or new equipment. For these reasons, we decided to try to design a low-cost patient monitor that would be easy to troubleshoot and reliable in the case of brownouts.

    Before COVID-19, we were able to construct a working pulse oximeter prototype, which is similar to the design by Scott Harden. We also made significant gains in the ECG module, but were unable to solder and put together the circuit to start testing because of the COVID-19 pandemic. However, we planned on synthesizing these two modules and using an Arduino for signal processing and data visualization. Because the main leads of the project (including me) have graduated, we intend to try to help the rising seniors in EWH finish our project.

  • 10/2017 - 12/2017
    ECE 110 Integrated Design Challenge
    Arduino robot that can maneuver through an obstacle course
    For ECE 110, Duke's introductory electrical engineering course, students were tasked with a half semester-long project in which groups of 2/3 needed to construct an Arduino robot that can maneuver through an obstacle course, complete specific tasks, and communicate to the rest of the other groups (about 4 other groups) information gained from each group's specific task.

    During my semester, our robot needed to navigate through a Star Wars themed course and relay to other groups how many cannons the Death Star had. To do this, we needed to scan for RFID tags. We needed to then communicate this number to other groups, and as a giant group, we needed to allow our robots to line up in a specific order based on the information we sent/received. Each semester, the class has a different obstacle course and theme. A demo for Fall 2019 is seen here.

  • 11/2015 - 03/2016
    Rubot
    LEGO Mindstorms Rubik's Cube Solver
    For my senior design project in high school, I decided to construct a LEGO Mindstorms robot that can solve a Rubik's cube. One of the objectives of this project is to integrate Java, a common programming language taught in the high school and middle school levels, with LEGO Mindstorms, something that is becoming commonly available in schools around the country. The purpose of this integration is to explore ways in which different secondary school classes can be integrated together.

    Rubot scraps the standard LEGO operating system with leJOS, which allows Java integration into LEGO Mindstorms. Rubot scans the Rubik's cube for its current configuration, and the Mindstorms block takes in this input and runs it through a solving algorithm. After constructing this virtual version of the solved cube, Rubot starts using servos to turn and solve the cube.
